ACL Injuries On The Rise In Young Women

A torn ACL is a common injury in athletes and those with active lifestyles, and is on a steady rise in young women in particular. Now more than ever, young female athletes are tearing their ACL’s or re-tearing them after surgery and physical therapy (Approximately 25% of women will retear them after surgery). In fact, this increasingly common injury has even affected celebrities such as Jamie Goodwin (So You Think You Can Dance), who has torn her ACL three times!

“More and more, I saw myself slipping out of alignment when I was dancing and jumping, and then one night I lay down and pushed myself up and my knee just popped right out,” Goodwin said. According to research by Goodwin’s surgeon, the main cause of re-injury is largely due in part to “complex neuromuscular control” or the way young girls have been hardwired to land and jump during athletic activity. On average, the femur is internally rotated, pushing the leg out of alignment and overloading the knee, often tearing the ACL.

Through improved physical therapy and rehab techniques, orthopaedic surgeons and physical therapists are helping to educate patients like Jamie to relearn how to land, jump, decelerate in a more safe fashion to increase chances of a recurring injury. In fact, according to Goodwin’s treating physician, he has seen an 88 percent reduction in recurring injuries through improved rehab techniques. What Is ACL Reconstruction?

Anterior Cruciate Ligament (ACL) surgery occurs as a result of a torn ACL ligament. A torn ACL can result in a loss of function after it’s injured and torn, resulting in a need to consider surgery to restore proper movement and function in the knee. According to the American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons, patients treated with surgical reconstruction of the ACL have long-term success rates of 82 percent to 95 percent. (http://orthoinfo.aaos.org/topic.cfm?topic=a00297)

How ACL Reconstruction Surgery Is Performed

ACL reconstruction surgery is done by removing the torn ligament from the area. Next, a graft is inserted to replace the ligament in an arthroscopic procedure (see diagram). There are three types of grafts that are most commonly used in arthroscopic surgery: autografts, allografts and the BEAR (bridge-enhanced ACL repair) procedure. The type of graft used is typically determined by patient age and lifestyle to decide which one would be most useful.

Our surgeons at Orthopaedic Specialists of Dallas prefer to prescribe arthroscopic surgery rather than open surgery for ACL injuries for the following reasons:

  • It is easy to see and work on the knee structures
  • It uses smaller incisions than open surgery
  • It can be done at the same time as diagnostic arthroscopy (using arthroscopy to find out about the injury or damage to the knee)
  • It may have less risks than open surgery

Arthroscopic surgery is done under regional (such as spinal) anesthesia or general anesthesia.

What Is Recovery Like?

Arthroscopic surgery has many benefits, including an easier healing time and there’s typically no need to stay overnight in a hospital like many other various surgeries. After your procedure, expect to feel tired for the first few days following your surgery. Swelling, bruising and numbness (especially around the incision on your knee,) are the most common ACL post-op symptoms and should begin to resolve within a few days and you’ll start to see and feel improvement in your knee. We do recommend icing the area operated on to reduce swelling and pain.

To care for your incision while it heals, you will need to keep it clean and dry and watch for signs of infection. As always, our surgeons and medical staff will give you detailed instructions about your post-recovery care and answer any questions you may have. What Should Be Expected During The Rehabilitation Process?

Physical rehabilitation needs after ACL surgery will vary with each patient. Typically, it takes about six months to be able to fully return back to any activity or participating in sports after his or her surgery.
